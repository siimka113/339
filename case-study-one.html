Dutch Design Week Microsite

Promoting select exhibitions by relating visitors personal experiences and the premise of exhibits.

Fall 2023

INTRO

A 7-week art direction and interaction design project for Dutch Design Week using insights from various precedent designers to guide the process. The final microsite promotes select exhibitions using a refined content strategy and user flow, tied together by highly-refined visual design and interactions.

This project is for a design class, IAT 235, and is not affiliated with Dutch Design Week.

OVERVIEW

The Client

Dutch Design Week (DDW) is one of the biggest design events in Europe,

featuring exhibitions, ceremonies, lectures, and festivities organized in over 100 different locations within Eindhoven, Netherlands.
Our exhibit selections are tied together by the common theme of confronting the future, prompting reconsideration of our relation to the world and values.


The Objective

Create an expressive and visually cohesive pre-exhibition microsite that encourages visitors to view exhibits in a memorable way.


FINAL SITE

Landing Page

Interactive Content Discovery

A hover to reveal image interaction that engages the visitor right away, giving them a sense of satisfaction when they uncover an image, which encourages them to explore further.

Elements of the page, such as text and images, dynamically move onto the screen based on scroll position, creating a seamless content discovery experience.


Explore Page

Linear Exhibition Presentation

I designed an automatic scroll interaction on the exhibition explore page, which ensures a preview of each exhibition is presented to the visitor, even if they are not actively scrolling through them.

The order of exhibits corresponds to a preset narrative arc that I outlined, which strings the themes of the exhibits together. While viewing in order is suggested, visitors can join the narrative at any point.


Exhibition Pages

An Introspective Journey

I created thought-provoking questions that introduce and frame each exhibit within a visitor’s life experience. This creates a personal connection between the visitor and the content, making them more likely to visit the exhibit.

At the bottom of each exhibit, a preview of the next one slides onto screen, coming into focus and scaling to frame. When clicked, the page transitions seamlessly to the next exhibition within the narrative.


CONCLUSION

“Don’t design in your head.” - Advice I heard repeatedly during the project, though I only recognized its importance in retrospect.

I learned the importance of having concrete design “evidence” to back up every decision, from high-level user flows to low-level visual design and interactive details. My approach to design changed significantly after completing this project, as I started thinking more intentionally about everything I presented to my teams, teaching staff, and stakeholders.
If I had more time, I would go back and refine the user flow to have a more flexible navigation system (instead of being purely linear), and experiment more with engaging or immersive interactions.
